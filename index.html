Road to beer expert

How do beer tastes evolve through time?

By: Pascal Epple, Thibaud Trinca, Léandre Castagna, Maxime Poffet

Group: rADAr

Introduction

From our own experience, we can tell that our beer tastes have drastically evolved over the past 10 years. At first, we only drank Pils and found other types of beer difficult to enjoy. Today, we can enjoy an IPA but are not yet satisfied when being served a Guinness. An obvious question occurs: are these shifts in beer tastes we experience common to other beer drinkers as well? By analyzing detailed beer ratings submitted by millions of users on two of the largest beer rating websites worldwide, namely BeerAdvocate and RateBeer, we want to determine if and how beer tastes of people evolve through time. To tackle this question, we explore and combine different data representation techniques. Moreover, using several graph mining algorithms, we detect properties of the network generated by different interactions users have with beers. Lastly, we mathematically consolidate our findings with the use of statistics.

Users filtering

For our results to be as meaningful as possible, we need to determine the proper subset of users on which we are going to conduct our analyses. As a matter of fact, not everyone posting an online review can be considered a beer expert and we need to impose some self-defined but yet solid criterias in order to be granted an expertise certificate. The filtering performed on the raw dataset can be divided into three different steps:

Market selection:

We decided to narrow down our research on users based in the United States. They represent the vast majority (73.6%) of the website’s active community. This selection makes users more comparable in the sense that they share a common drinking culture and should have access to the same products.

Temporal validity:

The number of reviews written by a user is not a solid enough criteria to ensure that he/she went through an evolution of his/her beer taste. In fact, there are several users that were highly active during a very short period of time. We therefore required the time difference between the last and the first review to be at least one year. Moreover, we noticed the presence of several organizations on the website that would usually release many reviews on the same day and hence decided to threshold the number of daily reviews to 20. The latter is also a small way to filter out eventual spam accounts.

Expertise criteria:

In combination with the above filters, we decided that users can be called experts as soon as they have submitted their 500th rating. Consequently, our analyses will focus on the evolution in the beer taste of users between their first and their 500th rating.

Clusters

As we want to see the evolution of similar users, we cluster them on the beginning of their "career". To do so, the K-means clustering is applied to the users represented as a 15-long vector containing the number of ratings in each categories. In doing so, the groups obtained represent users with similar starting tastes.

Choice of the number of clusters

As K-means requires the number of cluster to be set, we used the silhouette score to choose it in a way that will give well separated compact groups. By plotting the score for a reasonable range of cluster number, we can use the elbow method to determine the best K.
